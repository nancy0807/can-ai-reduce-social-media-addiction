# User journey map — Niharika

## What this map is

A minute-by-minute emotional and behavioral map of the compulsive
social media use cycle for the primary persona. Built from direct
self-observation, not assumptions. Every emotional note is verbatim
or directly paraphrased from the persona's own account.

The purpose of this map is not to describe behavior. It is to identify
the precise moments where an AI intervention has the highest leverage —
and what that intervention must do at each moment.

---

## The cycle

### Moment 1 — The intention
**Situation:** Morning or post-breakfast. A plan to study exists.
**Emotional state:** Neutral. Not excited, not dreading.
**Internal voice:** "I should study today."
**Body:** No tension. No urgency.

Key insight: The resistance does not exist yet. She is not starting
from a negative state. This means the problem is not motivational —
she has enough motivation to form the intention. What she lacks is
the bridge from intention to action.

AI opportunity: None yet. This moment does not need intervention.
Intervening here would feel intrusive and unnecessary.

---

### Moment 2 — The setup ritual
**Situation:** Books open, desk arranged, physically ready.
**Emotional state:** Transitional — neither here nor there.
**Internal voice:** "Okay let's start — but let me check my phone
real quick first."
**Body:** Hands busy with setup, mind already looking for an exit.

Key insight: The phone enters before the first word of studying.
The vulnerability window is not during a loading screen — it is
during the setup ritual itself. The act of preparing to study is
the trigger, not studying itself. Her brain recognises that effort
is about to be required and immediately seeks the exit.

This is anticipatory avoidance — escaping the predicted discomfort
of cognitive load before the load has actually arrived.

AI opportunity: HIGH. This is the first intervention point.
The "let me check real quick" thought is the cue firing.
The AI has approximately 10-15 seconds before the routine executes.

What the intervention must do:
- Acknowledge the setup (she is ready, that matters)
- Offer the smallest possible first step of the actual task
- Require zero decision-making from her
- Be faster to engage with than opening Instagram

What it must not do:
- Tell her not to check her phone
- Give her a motivational message
- Ask her a question that requires thought
- Add friction

Example intervention: "You're set up. First task: read the first
sentence of your notes from yesterday."
One action. No choice required. Ten seconds to comply.

---

### Moment 3 — The negotiation
**Situation:** Sitting at the desk, phone in hand or nearby.
**Emotional state:** Mild internal conflict — intention still present
but weakening.
**Internal voice:** "I'll study in a bit. Let me see one reel first."
**Body:** Relaxed, settling in. The decision feels reasonable.

Key insight: She has not abandoned the intention — she has deferred
it. "In a bit" is a genuine promise she is making to herself in
this moment. It does not feel like giving up. It feels like a
reasonable compromise. This is why willpower-based interventions
fail here — she does not feel like she needs willpower yet. She
feels like she is being kind to herself.

This is the negotiation phase — the last moment before the habit
loop fully executes and conscious decision-making exits the picture.

AI opportunity: MEDIUM. The window is closing but not closed.
An intervention here needs to be gentler than Moment 2 —
she has already partially committed to the scroll.

What the intervention must do:
- Not judge or guilt
- Acknowledge the "in a bit" without fighting it
- Plant a specific re-entry point: "When you come back, start here."
- Make the return feel easy in advance

Example intervention: "Okay. When you're ready, your starting
point is: [specific first line of task]. It'll take 2 minutes."
Pre-loading the re-entry reduces the wall she'll face in Moment 5.

---

### Moment 4 — The scroll
**Situation:** On Instagram, watching reels.
**Emotional state (first 30 seconds):** Genuine enjoyment.
Stimulation, mild pleasure, relief.
**Emotional state (after 10 minutes):** No distinct emotional state.
Awareness has dissolved. She is not deciding to continue.
She has stopped deciding altogether.
**Internal voice:** None. This is the absence of internal voice —
the default mode network has taken over.
**Body:** Still, passive, screen-lit face.

Key insight: The enjoyment is real in the first 30 seconds.
This is not a failure of character — the content is genuinely
stimulating and the relief from anticipatory fatigue is genuine.
An AI that tries to interrupt this moment will be resisted and
resented. The scroll itself is not the right intervention point.

After the first 30 seconds, she is no longer choosing to scroll.
She has entered what Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi would call an
inverted flow state — full absorption, but in a passive, low-effort
activity rather than a challenging one. Time disappears.

AI opportunity: LOW during scroll. Interrupting mid-scroll
triggers reactance and feels punishing.
The exception: a gentle time-marker at the 10-minute point —
not a block, not a guilt prompt, but a soft re-orientation:
"You've been resting for 10 minutes. Your starting point is
still waiting: [specific task line]."
Reminder of the pre-loaded re-entry from Moment 3.

---

### Moment 5 — The return (or the exit)
**Situation:** Phone down. Guilt present.
**Emotional state:** Self-discrepancy — painful gap between who
she intended to be (studying) and what just happened (scrolling).
**Internal voice:** Unspoken but present — shame, frustration,
the weight of time lost.
**Body:** Gets up. Leaves the room. Does not return to the desk.

Key insight: She does not transition from scroll back to studying.
She exits the situation entirely — physical movement as emotional
regulation. The guilt of the gap between ideal self and actual
self is more uncomfortable than starting the studying would have
been. So the nervous system solves the discomfort by changing
the environment.

This reveals a second wall — not just the study-initiation wall
from Moment 2, but a post-scroll re-entry wall that is emotionally
heavier than the first one. The cycle has made starting harder,
not easier.

AI opportunity: HIGH but delicate. This is the most emotionally
loaded moment in the cycle. The wrong intervention here
(guilt-amplifying, directive, urgent) will cause the user to
disengage from the product entirely.

What the intervention must do:
- Not mention the scroll or the time lost
- Not add to the guilt already present
- Offer a reset, not a reckoning
- Be extremely small and non-threatening
- Acknowledge the physical exit without judgment

Example intervention: "When you're ready to come back,
your starting point is one sentence. That's it."
No mention of Instagram. No mention of time. Just the door
left open, with a step small enough to walk through.

## The full cycle mapped

| Moment | Emotional state | AI opportunity | Intervention type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Intention | Neutral | None | — |
| 2. Setup ritual | Transitional, anticipatory | HIGH | Minimum viable first step |
| 3. Negotiation | Mild conflict, deferral | MEDIUM | Pre-load re-entry point |
| 4. Scroll — first 30s | Genuine enjoyment | None | — |
| 4. Scroll — 10min+ | Absent, dissolved | LOW | Soft time-marker + re-entry reminder |
| 5. Return/exit | Guilt, self-discrepancy | HIGH | Reset offer, no judgment |

---

## The critical design constraint this map reveals

There are two walls in this cycle, not one:

**Wall 1 — Study initiation wall (Moment 2)**
Anticipatory avoidance of cognitive load.
Intervention: minimum viable first step before the exit executes.

**Wall 2 — Post-scroll re-entry wall (Moment 5)**
Self-discrepancy and guilt making return feel heavier than the
original start would have been.
Intervention: judgment-free reset with pre-loaded starting point.

Most digital wellbeing products address neither wall.
They intervene during the scroll (Moment 4) — exactly when
the user is least receptive and most likely to resent the
interruption.

The AI must intervene at Moments 2, 3, and 5 — and stay
silent at Moments 1 and 4.

---

## What this means for Phase 3 — AI system design

The AI needs four capabilities derived from this journey map:

1. Setup detection — knows when the user has transitioned into
   a study/work context (desk setup signal, calendar event,
   to-do app state, or explicit user input)

2. Minimum viable action generation — can produce a single,
   specific, zero-decision first step for any task

3. Re-entry pre-loading — plants a specific return point during
   the negotiation phase so the post-scroll wall is lower

4. Judgment-free reset language — a distinct tone and vocabulary
   for Moment 5 that contains zero guilt amplification

## Open questions for root cause analysis

- How does the AI detect Moment 2 (setup ritual) without
  requiring the user to manually signal it every time?
- What is the minimum data the AI needs to generate a
  task-specific first step? (Full task list? Just the subject?)
- How does the reset intervention in Moment 5 avoid feeling
  patronising or hollow after repeated use?
- Does the cycle repeat multiple times per day — and if so,
  does the intervention need to adapt across repetitions?
